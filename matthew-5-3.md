# Matthew 5:3

## Original Language (Greek)

> Μακάριοι οἱ πτωχοὶ τῷ πνεύματι, ὅτι αὐτῶν ἐστιν ἡ βασιλεία τῶν οὐρανῶν.

**Transliteration:** Makarioi hoi ptōchoi tō pneumati, hoti autōn estin hē basileia tōn ouranōn.

**Literal word-for-word:** Blessed the poor in-the spirit, because of-them is the kingdom of-the heavens.

## Translation Analysis

### The stubborn consistency — and what it hides

This is another verse where English translations are nearly identical across 600 years. But this consistency hides a fierce debate about what **"poor in spirit"** actually means — a debate the Greek doesn't fully resolve, and translators have chosen not to take sides.

### μακάριοι (makarioi) — "Blessed" carries more weight than it seems

**Makarioi** is almost universally rendered **"Blessed"**, but the Greek word has a richer sense than the English suggests. In classical Greek, *makarios* described the state of the gods — a transcendent happiness beyond ordinary human experience, independent of circumstances. It wasn't "happy" in the modern sense (feeling good), but something closer to **"deeply fortunate in a way the world can't touch."**

Some modern paraphrases attempt alternatives — "How happy are" (Phillips), "You're blessed when" (The Message) — but every serious translation sticks with "Blessed," accepting the limitation. The word has become so fused with the Beatitudes in English that translators treat it as essentially untranslatable and rely on the reader's accumulated understanding.

### πτωχοί (ptōchoi) — "poor" doesn't go far enough

The Greek **ptōchoi** doesn't mean merely poor (lacking money). The word specifically describes the **utterly destitute** — a beggar who has nothing and depends entirely on others for survival. Greek had another word for ordinary poor people (*penēs* — someone who works hard but has little). Jesus chose the more extreme word.

**"Poor in spirit"** therefore doesn't mean "feeling a little humble." It means **spiritually destitute** — recognizing you have absolutely nothing to offer God, no spiritual merit, no righteousness of your own. You are a beggar before God. Every translation uses "poor," which in modern English sounds mild. The Greek is not mild.

### "in spirit" — whose spirit?

The phrase **τῷ πνεύματι** (tō pneumati — "in the spirit") is ambiguous in Greek. It could mean:

1. **"poor in their own spirit"** — humble, recognizing their spiritual bankruptcy (the dominant Protestant reading)
2. **"poor by the Holy Spirit"** — made poor/emptied by God's Spirit as a spiritual discipline
3. **"poor in the realm of spirit"** — contrasting spiritual poverty with material wealth (connecting to Luke's parallel: "Blessed are you who are poor" — Luke 6:20, no "in spirit" qualifier)

Every English translation preserves the ambiguity with the literal "poor in spirit" and lets the reader decide. This is one of those rare cases where the translators' refusal to interpret is itself an interpretation — they're saying the ambiguity is the point.

### "kingdom of heaven" — Matthew's Jewish circumlocution

Matthew consistently writes **"kingdom of heaven"** (βασιλεία τῶν οὐρανῶν) where the other Gospels say "kingdom of God." This reflects Jewish reverence for God's name — avoiding saying "God" directly by substituting "heaven." Every translation preserves this distinction faithfully, rendering Matthew's phrase as-is rather than harmonizing it with Mark and Luke.
